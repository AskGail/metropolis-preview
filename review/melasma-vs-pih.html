Melasma vs. dark spots from a breakout: how to tell them apart.

Two of the most common pigment problems on deeper skin look almost identical, and they are treated very differently. Telling them apart is the step that decides whether treatment works, or quietly makes things worse.

Why this one distinction matters so much

If you have brown or grey-brown patches on deeper skin, there is a good chance you have wondered whether it is melasma or the mark left behind by an old breakout. It is a fair question, because to the eye they can look the same. But they come from different places, behave differently over time, and respond to different treatment. Get the two confused and the plan that helps one can worsen the other.

On skin of color this matters more than on lighter skin, for one reason: both conditions are driven, in part, by pigment cells that overreact, and both can be made worse by anything that inflames the skin. Treating the wrong one aggressively is how people end up with more pigment than they started with. So the honest first step is not a product. It is figuring out which one you actually have.

What each one actually is

Melasma

Melasma is a light- and hormone-driven pattern of pigment. It tends to appear symmetrically, on the cheeks, forehead, upper lip, and bridge of the nose, without any injury coming first. It is chronic and relapsing, which means it is managed over time rather than cured in a course. Our full melasma guide covers how it is treated →

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)

PIH is pigment left behind after the skin has been inflamed or injured, a breakout, a bug bite, eczema, an ingrown hair, a scratch, or a cosmetic procedure that was too harsh. It shows up exactly where the insult was, in the shape of whatever caused it. Unlike melasma, PIH often fades on its own over months once the inflammation is gone, though on deeper skin it can be stubborn and take longer.

The tell that helps most

Ask what came first. If a breakout, a rash, or an injury sat there before the mark appeared, it is probably PIH. If the patches arrived on their own, symmetrically, and track with sun and hormones, it points to melasma.

How a dermatologist tells them apart

The history is the biggest clue, but it is not the only one, and on deeper skin the stakes are too high to guess. A dermatologist reads several things together:

  • The story. Was there a breakout, rash, or procedure at that exact spot first? PIH follows an insult; melasma does not.
  • The distribution. Symmetrical patches across the face suggest melasma; marks that map to old blemishes suggest PIH.
  • A closer look. Tools like a Wood's lamp or dermoscopy can help judge how deep the pigment sits, which affects how, and how quickly, it will respond. This read is a guide, not a verdict, which is part of why an in-person exam matters.

They can also happen together

This is the part that makes self-diagnosis risky: melasma and PIH often coexist on the same face, and a melasma patch can pick up PIH on top of it if it is treated too aggressively. When both are present, the plan has to address each without provoking the other. That balance is difficult to strike from a bathroom shelf, and easy to get wrong with a strong product or an aggressive in-office treatment.

Why the label changes the treatment

Once the diagnosis is clear, the two plans diverge. Melasma is managed with light and visible-light protection, careful topicals, and conservative in-office steps, over the long term. PIH is treated first by calming whatever is inflaming the skin (getting the acne or eczema under control), and only then by fading the marks, gently, because anything harsh can restart the very inflammation that caused them.

What both share, on skin of color, is the same golden rule: avoid triggering more pigment than you resolve. That is why the right first move for either one is the same, an accurate diagnosis, then a plan built for your skin. See the full melasma treatment ladder →

Common questions

Can I have both melasma and PIH at the same time?

Yes, and it is common on deeper skin. They can overlap on the same area, which is one reason self-diagnosis is unreliable and an in-person exam is worth it. Dr. Higgins: confirm.

Will dark spots from a breakout go away on their own?

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation often fades over months once the underlying inflammation is controlled, though on deeper skin it can be slow and stubborn. Protecting the skin and treating the cause speeds it up; picking or harsh products slow it down. Dr. Higgins: confirm timeline framing.

Is one harder to treat than the other?

They are different, not simply harder or easier. PIH usually improves once its cause is calmed; melasma is chronic and managed over time. What they share on skin of color is that aggressive treatment can worsen either one. Dr. Higgins: confirm.
