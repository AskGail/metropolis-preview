01 · What it is

What melasma is, and why deeper skin sees more of it

If you are reading this, there is a fair chance you have already tried to treat this yourself. A product, maybe several. Maybe a treatment somewhere that did not hold, or left you with more pigment than you started with. That is the common story with melasma on deeper skin, and it is not a failure on your part. Melasma punishes the wrong approach, which is exactly why the right one starts with understanding what it actually is.

Melasma shows up as symmetrical patches of brown or grey-brown, usually on the cheeks, forehead, upper lip, and bridge of the nose, the areas that catch the most light. It is centered on pigment-producing cells, called melanocytes, that deposit extra melanin, but it is better understood as a whole-skin response: sunlight, hormones, heat, and inflammation all feed those cells and keep them switched on. That is why it behaves less like a stain on the surface and more like a sensitivity the skin holds onto.

On skin of color those cells are already more active by nature. That is what gives deeper skin its richness, and it is also why melasma appears more often here, and holds on longer, than it does on the lightest skin. The same biology that protects deeper skin from the sun is the biology that, once provoked, makes pigment harder to settle back down.

The short version

Melasma is not a stain you can scrub away. It is overactive pigment responding to triggers, so the real work is calming the trigger and the cell, not attacking the skin.

Two things follow, and they shape everything below. First, melasma is treatable but not, in the honest sense, curable. It is controlled and maintained, the way you would manage any tendency the skin keeps. Second, on deeper skin the margin for error is thin: the aggressive treatments that clear pigment fastest on light skin are the same ones most likely to leave a darker mark behind. Restraint is not the cautious option here. It is the technique.

02 · Getting the diagnosis right

Melasma, or something that looks like it

The most important step happens before any treatment, and it is the one most often skipped: making sure the pigment is actually melasma. On deeper skin several things look alike to the eye and behave very differently.

  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is pigment left behind by a prior insult, a breakout, a scratch, a cosmetic procedure that was too aggressive. It is extremely common on skin of color, and it responds to a different, gentler approach than melasma. Treating melasma with something that inflames the skin can create PIH on top of it, which is how people end up worse than they started. How to tell melasma and PIH apart →
  • Where the pigment sits matters. Melasma can be mostly in the upper layers of the skin, mostly deeper down, or a mix of both. Deeper pigment is more resistant to topical treatment, and pretending otherwise sets a false expectation. A dermatologist can read this at the visit, sometimes with a simple Wood's lamp, and it changes what we recommend.
  • Other pigment conditions, from lichen planus pigmentosus to a medication-related pigmentation, can mimic melasma and need a different route entirely. This is exactly the distinction a board-certified dermatologist is trained to make, and a walk-in aesthetic menu is not.

This is the first place experience earns its keep. Get the diagnosis wrong on deeper skin and the treatment does not just fail, it can add a second problem to the one you came in with.

03 · What sets it off

The triggers, and the one most people miss

Melasma is a response, so controlling it starts with the things that provoke it. The usual triggers are real: ultraviolet sun exposure, hormones (pregnancy, birth control, and hormone therapy are common starting points), and heat. But the trigger most people, and a surprising number of products, miss is visible light, the ordinary light from the sky, a window, and screens.

This is the detail that changes outcomes on skin of color. Visible light drives pigment more strongly in deeper skin than in lighter skin, and a standard clear sunscreen does very little against it. That single fact reshapes the foundation of any real melasma plan, which is why it leads the treatment section rather than sitting as an afterthought.

Why it keeps coming back

If the light exposure driving the pigment is never addressed, every treatment underneath it is working against a tap that is still running. Protection is not the boring part. It is the part that makes the rest hold.

04 · Treatment

The treatment ladder, built for deeper skin

There is no single melasma treatment. There is a sequence, and on skin of color the order matters as much as the ingredients. We build from the gentlest, most protective layer up, and add intensity only when it is earned and only when the skin has shown it can take it.

Foundation: protection you can see working

Every plan starts here, and skipping it is why so many melasma treatments quietly fail. On deeper skin that means broad-spectrum sun protection that also blocks visible light, which in practice means a tinted sunscreen with iron oxides. The tint is not cosmetic. It is the active defense against the visible light that drives pigment in skin of color, and a clear sunscreen does not provide it. Worn daily, reapplied, and paired with shade and a hat where it counts, this is the layer the rest of the plan rests on. Why tinted sunscreen matters for deeper skin tones →

Topicals: the workhorses

Most melasma is managed, first and for the long term, with topical treatment. These are the tools our dermatologists reach for, each chosen for the skin in front of them rather than off a template.

In-office, done carefully

Procedures can help, but this is where skin of color demands the most restraint, and where the wrong hands do the most damage. The rule our dermatologists work by is simple: never provoke more pigment than you resolve.

  • Chemical peels, chosen conservatively (the gentler acids at controlled strengths), can lift surface pigment. The deeper, more aggressive peels that are routine on light skin are approached with real caution here, because they can trigger PIH. Chemical peels on deeper skin, done safely →
  • Lasers and energy devices are used selectively and at low, careful settings, if at all. Some approaches, such as low-fluence laser toning, have a role in trained hands, but they also carry a risk of rebound pigment or patchy lightening on deeper skin, so they are never a first move. Just as important is what we do not do: the aggressive resurfacing and broad light-based devices known to backfire on melasma in skin of color.
  • Microneedling is sometimes combined with topical pigment tools to help them penetrate, again at a measured pace.

05 · What to avoid

The mistakes that make melasma worse

Because the wrong treatment can add pigment rather than remove it, knowing what to avoid is as valuable as knowing what to try. On deeper skin, these cause the most regret:

  • Aggressive lasers and strong light-based treatments marketed as a fast fix. On melasma in skin of color, the fast fix is often the thing that leaves a lasting mark.
  • Unsupervised, open-ended hydroquinone from wherever it can be bought. It works when it is prescribed, timed, and monitored, and it causes its own problems when it is not.
  • Anything that inflames the skin, harsh scrubs, over-exfoliation, a too-strong peel from a non-medical setting. Inflammation is a trigger, so irritation is not a sign it is working, it is a setback in progress.
  • Treating without a diagnosis. If no one confirmed it was melasma, the treatment is a guess, and on deeper skin a guess can cost you the very thing you came in to fix.

If a treatment promises to erase melasma in one session, that is the clearest signal to slow down. Melasma does not work that way.

06 · What to expect

An honest picture of results, cost, and upkeep

Melasma responds well to a patient, well-built approach, and it tends to return when that approach stops. This is not a failure of treatment, it is the nature of the condition, and hearing it up front is part of good care.

Expect gradual progress measured over weeks and months, not days. Expect the foundation, the daily protection, to stay in place long after the pigment fades, because it is what keeps it faded.

A word on cost, because most people arrive having already spent. On products that did not hold, sometimes on a treatment that made things worse. A correct plan is usually less than the trial and error it replaces, because it stops the cycle of paying to guess. We will be straight with you about what care involves before you commit to any of it.

And the upkeep is not a treadmill you walk alone. It is a relationship. We stay with you, ease off the stronger tools once your skin is clear, and step back in if it flares. Managed this way, melasma becomes something handled with a team that knows your skin, on your terms, rather than something you fight by yourself.

The realistic goal

Not one dramatic before-and-after, but clear, even skin that stays that way, because the protection behind it never fully stops. That is a result you can keep.

08 · Common questions

Common questions about melasma on skin of color

Can melasma be cured?

Melasma is controlled rather than cured. With the right approach it can fade substantially and stay quiet, but it tends to return if the daily protection and maintenance stop, which is why we build a plan you can actually keep up. Dr. Higgins: confirm this framing.

Is laser safe for melasma on deeper skin tones?

Sometimes, in trained hands and at conservative settings, but it is never a first step and never the whole plan. Some lasers can trigger rebound pigment or uneven lightening on deeper skin, so we treat energy devices as a careful, later option and avoid the aggressive ones entirely. Dr. Higgins: confirm which devices you use or avoid for melasma on deeper skin.

What is the difference between melasma and dark spots from a breakout?

Dark spots left by a breakout or irritation are post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, common on skin of color and usually improved with a gentler approach. Melasma is a separate, hormone- and light-driven pattern that behaves differently. Telling them apart is the first thing we do, because they are treated differently. Dr. Higgins: confirm.

Will hydroquinone lighten my natural skin tone?

Used as prescribed, in defined courses and under a dermatologist's supervision, it lightens the overactive pigment, not your natural skin tone. The concern on deeper skin is long, unsupervised use, which is why we prescribe it deliberately and monitor it. Dr. Higgins: confirm.

Is it safe to treat melasma during pregnancy?

Some treatments are set aside in pregnancy and others, such as azelaic acid and diligent protection, are generally considered appropriate, but this is a decision to make with your dermatologist and your obstetrician, not from a page. We build a pregnancy-safe plan when that is the situation. Dr. Higgins: confirm the pregnancy-safe framing.

How long until I see a difference?

Most people see gradual change over several weeks to a few months, not days. Melasma responds to consistency, so the plan is built to be something you can keep up. Dr. Higgins: confirm typical timeline.
